Macbeth: Act V Quick Guide

Literal Meaning and Analytical Foundations

Act V, Scene 4

Summary (What Literally Happens)

Malcolm's English forces and the Scottish rebels meet in Birnam Wood. Malcolm orders his soldiers to cut down branches and carry them in front of them as camouflage. This action serves two purposes: to disguise the actual size of their army from Macbeth's scouts and to make the final prophecy—that Macbeth is safe until "Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane Hill / Shall come"—seem possible, even if Macbeth does yet not know about the movement. Siward, the English general, remarks that they must wait to fight until they reach Macbeth's castle, where they will meet their fate.

Analysis (Interpretation & Meaning)

This scene confirms the first prophecy's deceptive nature. The ambiguity of the witches' words is revealed—the Wood is not moving supernaturally, but is being carried by men. This is the first practical sign that Macbeth's overconfidence, based on the equivocation of the apparitions, is misplaced. It shows Malcolm's strategic intelligence and restores a sense of natural, military order against the chaos Macbeth created. The stage is literally set for the final battle and Macbeth's doom.

Act V, Scene 6

Summary (What Literally Happens)

Malcolm, Siward, and Macduff reach the plains just outside Dunsinane Castle, dropping the branches they used for cover. Malcolm immediately gives the order for Siward and his son, young Siward, to lead the first assault. Macduff, however, insists that he will lead the vanguard (the front line) and that the trumpets should sound the order for the attack. This brief scene is entirely dedicated to the military preparations for the final confrontation.

Analysis (Interpretation & Meaning)

This scene acts as a rapid, tense transition, focusing the audience's attention entirely on the approaching violence. Macduff's desire to lead the charge highlights his personal and passionate need for vengeance against Macbeth, who murdered his family. The immediate command for action, represented by the "loud alarums," contrasts sharply with the earlier scenes of paranoia and slow mental decay in Macbeth's castle. Order and justice are now literally knocking at the gates of tyranny.

Key Quotes for Analysis

Quote (Scene 4): "Let every soldier hew him down a bough / And bear't before him. Thereby shall we shadow / The numbers of our host and make discovery / Err in report of us."

Significance: This is Malcolm's military strategy that perfectly fulfills the "moving forest" prophecy. It shows the difference between the noble, tactical leadership of Malcolm and the rash, desperate tyranny of Macbeth. The supernatural is defeated by pragmatic reason.

Quote (Scene 6): "We have met with foes / That strike beside us."

Significance: Malcolm notes that many of Macbeth's own men are fighting poorly or deliberately missing their targets (striking "beside" the enemy), indicating that Macbeth's army is disloyal and demoralized. This shows the internal rot of Macbeth's reign—even those sworn to him no longer believe in his cause.

Quote (Scene 6): "Make all our trumpets speak; give them all breath, / Those clamorous harbingers of blood and death."

Significance: Macduff is eager for the final battle. The loud, aggressive sound of the trumpets ("clamorous harbingers") symbolizes the arrival of justice and the end of the psychological terror that has dominated the play. It marks the shift from internal drama to external, physical warfare.

Foundations for Analytical Writing

These core interpretations and questions will help you move from reading the plot to writing deep literary analysis.

1. Ambition & The Tragic Hero

Concept: Macbeth is a classic Tragic Hero whose excessive ambition (hamartia) leads to his catastrophic downfall. This ambition is powerful because he is fully aware of the evil he commits.

  • Key Question: How does Shakespeare use Macbeth's soliloquies (his inner speeches) to show the audience that he knows he is doing wrong, but chooses to proceed anyway? This demonstrates his moral awareness.
  • Analytical Task: Trace the language of desire and fear in Act I, comparing it to the cold apathy and despair Macbeth expresses in Act V (e.g., the "Tomorrow, and tomorrow" speech).
2. Appearance vs. Reality (Equivocation)

Concept: The play revolves around deception, particularly the Witches' equivocations—statements with double meanings designed to mislead. Macbeth's failure is believing the literal surface meaning instead of the hidden context.

  • Key Question: How do the phrases "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" (Witches) and "look like the innocent flower, / But be the serpent under’t" (Lady Macbeth) apply to characters other than the main villains? Consider Malcolm's military disguise in Scene 4.
  • Analytical Task: Write a short paragraph on how a specific character (Macbeth, Lady Macbeth, or the Witches) weaponizes language to twist or conceal the truth, forcing the audience to constantly question what is real.
3. Nature vs. Unnatural/Cosmic Disorder

Concept: Regicide breaks the Chain of Being (the divine natural order). Shakespeare illustrates this cosmic rupture through dramatic weather and unnatural events (pathetic fallacy).

  • Key Question: What happens in the natural world immediately after Duncan's murder (Act II, Scene 4)? What natural imagery does Malcolm use at the play's end (referencing "planting") to suggest he is restoring order to Scotland?
  • Analytical Task: Structure an essay around this theme: Thesis: Macbeth's evil corrupts both the political and natural worlds. Body Paragraphs: 1. Political Corruption (Regicide/Tyranny). 2. Natural Corruption (Storms/Disorderly Animals). 3. Restoration of Order (Malcolm’s return and resolution).

Act V, Scene 4: Birnam Wood

Drum and Colours. Enter MALCOLM, SIWARD, MACDUFF, MALCOLM’S Son, MENTEITH, CAITHNESS, ANGUS, and Soldiers, marching.

MALCOLM: Cousins, I hope the days are near at hand

That call us to a just and peaceful state.

MENTEITH: We doubt it nothing.

SIWARD: What wood is this before us?

MENTEITH: The wood of Birnam.

MALCOLM: Let every soldier hew him down a bough

And bear't before him. Thereby shall we shadow

The numbers of our host and make discovery

Err in report of us.

SOLDIER: It shall be done.

SIWARD: The rather, if you please, draw near enough

To make our great opinion of success

Clear in the doubt of battle.

CAITHNESS: The sight of us will cheer the loyal hearts.

MALCOLM: We learn no other but the confident tyrant

Keeps still in Dunsinane, and will endure

Our setting down before 't.

MENTEITH: 'Tis his main hope;

For where there is advantage to be taken,

Both more and less have given him the revolt,

And none serve with him but constrained things

Whose hearts are absent too.

MACDUFF: Let our just censures attend the true event,

And put we on industrious soldiership.

SIWARD: The time approaches

That will with due decision make us know

What we shall say we have and what we owe.

Thoughts speculative their unsure hopes relate,

But certain issue strokes must arbitrate:

Towards which advance the war.

[Exeunt, marching.]

Act V, Scene 6: Near Dunsinane

Drum and Colours. Enter MALCOLM, SIWARD, MACDUFF, and their Army, with Boughs.

MALCOLM: Now near enough;

Your leavy screens throw down

And show like those you are. You, worthy uncle,

Shall, with my cousin, your right-noble son,

Lead our first battle. Worthy Macduff and we

Shall take the chance of that high consequence,

Ay, break the business down.

SIWARD: Fare you well.

Do we but find the tyrant's power tonight,

Let us be beaten if we cannot fight.

MACDUFF: Make all our trumpets speak; give them all breath,

Those clamorous harbingers of blood and death.

[Exeunt. Alarums.]
